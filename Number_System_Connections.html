1 · What Is a Rational Number?

Everything in this unit lives inside one big set: the rational numbers.

By the end: you can classify, compare and order positive and negative rational numbers.

The definition

A rational number is any number that can be written as a fraction a/b, where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0.

  • Integers count: -5 = -5/1, and 0 = 0/1.
  • Fractions count: 3/4, -7/2.
  • Terminating & repeating decimals count: 0.25 = 1/4, 0.333… = 1/3.
  • Each one has a positive and a negative version. The number line stretches both ways from 0.
Watch the disguises: 12/3 looks like a fraction but equals the integer 4. Always simplify before you decide what kind of number you have.

2 · The Number Line & Absolute Value

Opposites, absolute value, and distance — all read straight off the line.

By the end: you can find opposites and absolute value, and the distance between two points.

Three ideas to lock in

  • Opposite (additive inverse): same distance from 0, other side. The opposite of -7 is 7.
  • Absolute value |x|: the distance from 0, always zero or positive. |-6| = 6.
  • Distance between two points: |a - b|. From -3 to 5 is |5 - (-3)| = 8.
Distance from -3 to 5 is 8 units: |5 - (-3)| = |8| = 8.

3 · Rational Numbers as Decimals

Every fraction becomes a decimal that either stops or repeats forever.

By the end: you can convert between fractions and decimals and tell terminating from repeating.

Terminating vs repeating

Divide the numerator by the denominator. The result terminates (stops) or repeats (a block of digits cycles forever).

The shortcut: reduce the fraction first. If the denominator's only prime factors are 2 and/or 5, the decimal terminates. Any other prime factor (3, 7, 11, …) → it repeats.

Examples: 3/8 has denominator 8 = 2³ → terminates (0.375). 1/6 has a factor of 3 → repeats (0.1666…).

4 · Adding & Subtracting Integers

The foundation move of the whole unit — get the signs automatic.

By the end: you can add and subtract integers fluently, including double signs.

The rules, and why they work

  • Additive inverses sum to zero: -9 + 9 = 0. A number and its opposite cancel.
  • Same signs: add the values, keep the sign. -6 + (-9) = -15.
  • Different signs: subtract the smaller from the larger value, keep the sign of the larger. 8 + (-15) = -7.
  • Subtracting is adding the opposite: 5 - (-8) = 5 + 8 = 13.
Two signs in a row? Turn them into one. - (-) becomes +; + (-) becomes -.

6 · Multiplying & Dividing Rational Numbers

One clean sign rule covers both operations.

By the end: you can multiply and divide rational numbers using the sign rules.

The sign rule

  • Same signs → positive. -8 × (-5) = 40, -36 ÷ (-9) = 4.
  • Different signs → negative. -6 × 7 = -42, 48 ÷ (-6) = -8.
  • Count the negatives: an even number of negative factors → positive; an odd number → negative.
  • Dividing by a fraction = multiplying by its reciprocal.
