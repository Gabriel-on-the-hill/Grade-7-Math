1 · Scale Drawings

A scale tells you how many real units one drawn unit stands for.

By the end: you can find real lengths and areas from a scale drawing, and redraw one at a new scale.

Reading a scale

  • A scale 1 cm : 4 m means every 1 cm drawn is 4 m real.
  • Drawn → real: multiply. Real → drawn: divide.
  • Areas do not scale the same way. If lengths are ×4, areas are ×16 — because area uses two dimensions.
Worked example — a room at 1 cm : 4 m

On the drawing the room is 5.5 cm by 3 cm.

Real length: 5.5 × 4 = 22 m. Real width: 3 × 4 = 12 m.

Real area: 22 × 12 = 264 m². Notice the drawn area was 16.5 cm², and 16.5 × 16 = 264 — the square of the scale factor.

2 · Building Triangles

Some sets of measurements fix exactly one triangle. Some allow many. Some allow none at all.

By the end: you can decide whether given measurements make a unique triangle, more than one, or none.

Three outcomes, and how to tell

  • Triangle inequality: the two shorter sides must add to more than the longest. Equal is not enough — that lies flat.
  • Three sides (SSS) that pass the test → exactly one triangle.
  • Three angles summing to 180° → infinitely many — same shape, any size.
Worked example — sides 4, 5, 12

Add the two shorter sides: 4 + 5 = 9. Compare with the longest: 9 < 12.

The short sides cannot reach across the long one, so no triangle can be built. Try it with sides 4, 5, 8 instead: 9 > 8, and exactly one triangle closes up.

GuidedDoes it close?
Q · Can a triangle have sides 3, 4 and 8?
The two shorter sides together are shorter than the longest, so they cannot meet. No triangle.
PracticeSelect all that work
Q · Select all the side sets that form a triangle.
For each set, add the two shorter sides and compare with the longest — the sum has to be more than the longest, not just equal to it.
ApplyHow many triangles?
Q · A triangle must have angles 60°, 60° and 60°. How many different triangles fit?
The angles add to 180°, so triangles exist — but nothing fixes the size. Angles alone give you the shape; you need at least one side to pin down which triangle.
ExamExam-style · unique, many, or none
A triangle has sides 8 cm, 15 cm and 17 cm. How many such triangles exist?
8 + 15 = 23 > 17, so it closes. Three fixed side lengths leave no freedom at all — the triangle is unique (up to sliding or turning it).

3 · Slicing Solids

Cut through a 3-D solid and the flat face you expose is a 2-D shape.

By the end: you can name the two-dimensional shape produced by slicing a prism, a pyramid or a cylinder.

What the cut leaves behind

  • Parallel to the base of a prism or cylinder: the slice matches the base exactly.
  • Parallel to the base of a pyramid: the same shape as the base, but smaller.
  • Perpendicular to the base (a vertical cut): usually a rectangle.
Worked example — a rectangular prism, cut flat

Slice a right rectangular prism with a horizontal cut. Every such slice is a rectangle the same size as the base — the prism is that base, repeated all the way up.

Now stand the cut upright instead, straight down through the solid: that face is a rectangle too, but its dimensions come from the height and one base edge.

Cut parallel to the base of a prism and the face you expose matches the base exactly.
GuidedCutting a prism
Q · A right rectangular prism is sliced parallel to its base. What shape is the slice?
A prism is its base repeated at every height, so a horizontal slice reproduces the base exactly.
PracticeCutting a pyramid
Q · A right rectangular pyramid is sliced parallel to its base, halfway up. What shape is the slice?
A pyramid narrows towards its apex, so a parallel slice keeps the base’s shape but shrinks it.
ApplyCutting a cylinder
Q · A right circular cylinder is sliced vertically, straight through the centre. What shape is exposed?
Horizontally you would get a circle. Cut straight down through the middle and you get a rectangle: the height of the cylinder by the full diameter.

4 · Circles: Circumference & Area

Two different questions about a circle — how far around, and how much inside.

By the end: you can find circumference and area from a radius or a diameter, and say which one a problem needs.

The two formulas, and how they relate

  • Circumference (distance around): C = 2πr, or C = πd.
  • Area (space inside): A = πr².
  • π is the same for every circle: C ÷ d ≈ 3.14. Use 3.14 here.
  • Why they are related: cut a circle into thin wedges and lay them alternately — they form a near-rectangle of height r and width half the circumference. So A = (1/2) × C × r, which is exactly πr².
Worked example — a circle of radius 7

C = 2 × 3.14 × 7 = 43.96 units around.

A = 3.14 × 7² = 3.14 × 49 = 153.86 square units.

Different units, different questions: fencing a round garden needs C; turfing it needs A.

Radius to the edge, diameter all the way across: d = 2r.
GuidedAround the circle
Step 1 · A circle has radius 5 cm. Circumference? (Use π = 3.14.)
C =cm
C = 2πr — double the radius first, then multiply by 3.14.
Step 2 · A circle has diameter 10 cm. Area? (Use π = 3.14.)
A =cm²
Area needs the radius. Halve the diameter before you square it.
PracticeWhich formula?
Q · A gardener wants to put edging all the way around a circular flower bed. Which does she need?
Edging runs around the bed, so it is a length: circumference. Area would tell her how much soil or turf to buy instead.

5 · Angle Relationships as Equations

Angle facts turn a figure into an equation you can solve.

By the end: you can use supplementary, complementary, vertical and adjacent angle facts to write and solve an equation.

Four facts worth knowing cold

  • Complementary: two angles adding to 90°.
  • Supplementary: two angles adding to 180° — a straight line.
  • Vertical angles (opposite, where two lines cross) are equal.
  • Adjacent angles share a side; if they fill a known angle, they add to it.
Worked example — write it, then solve it

Two supplementary angles measure (2x + 10)° and 40°.

Supplementary means they add to 180: 2x + 10 + 40 = 180.

2x + 50 = 180 → 2x = 130 → x = 65. Check: 2(65) + 10 = 140, and 140 + 40 = 180 ✓

Vertical angles (opposite each other) are equal; neighbours on a straight line add to 180°.

6 · Area, Surface Area & Volume

How much space a solid holds, and how much material covers it.

By the end: you can find the volume and surface area of prisms and cylinders, including composite shapes.

Two ideas cover almost everything

  • Volume of any prism or cylinder: V = B × h, where B is the area of the base.
  • Surface area: add up the area of every face. For a box, opposite faces match, so SA = 2(lw + lh + wh).
  • Volume is measured in cubic units, surface area in square units — the units tell you which you found.
Worked example — a box 8 by 5 by 3

Volume. Base area B = 8 × 5 = 40; height 3. V = 40 × 3 = 120 cubic units.

Surface area. 2(8 × 5 + 8 × 3 + 5 × 3) = 2(40 + 24 + 15) = 2 × 79 = 158 square units.

GuidedVolume of a prism
Step 1 · A rectangular prism is 6 cm by 4 cm by 10 cm. Volume?
V =cm³
Find the area of one base first, then multiply by how tall the solid stands.
Step 2 · Surface area of that same prism?
SA =cm²
Three different face sizes, each appearing twice.

ExamMCAP · surface area of a solid
At each vertex, three line segments intersect at right angles.
MCAP item · The figure shows a solid. At each vertex, three line segments intersect at right angles. What is the surface area, in square feet, of the solid? Select one answer.
Two L-shaped faces: each is 4 × 4 with a 2 × 2 notch removed, so 16 - 4 = 12 ft² each, giving 24 ft². The band wrapping around them has height 2 ft and length equal to the L’s perimeter, 4 + 4 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 16 ft, giving 32 ft². Total 24 + 32 = 56 ft². The notch adds surface even though it removes volume — that is what makes 36 tempting and wrong.

7 · Module Check — Show What You Know

Mixed, exam-style. No new ideas — everything here has appeared above.

By the end: you can pick the right tool without being told which section a question came from.

Before you start

  • Read for what is being asked: a distance around, a surface covered, or a space filled?
  • Check your units. cm, cm² and cm³ answer different questions.
Worked example — choosing the tool

“How much wrapping paper covers this box?” → surface area. “How much sand fills it?” → volume. “How much ribbon goes round it?” → a length.

The numbers in the question are often identical. The question is what tells you which formula to reach for.

ExamExam-style · find the error
To find the area of a circle of diameter 10 cm, a student computes 3.14 × 10² = 314. What went wrong?
The formula takes the radius, not the diameter. Using d where r belongs makes the answer 4 times too big — and 314 ÷ 4 = 78.5, which is exactly the gap.

ExamMCAP · how many tiles
MCAP item · A room floor will be covered with ceramic tiles. The length of the room is 12 feet, the width is 2/3 of the length, and the tiles are squares with 1½ inches per side. Which expression represents the number of ceramic tiles that will be needed?
Work in inches. The floor is 12 ft by 2/3 × 12 = 8 ft; in inches that is (12 × 12) by (12 × 2/3 × 12), so the floor area is their product. Each tile is 3/2 in square, area 3/2 × 3/2. The number of tiles is floor area ÷ tile area — that is A, and it works out to 6{,}144. Multiplying instead (B, D) uses the wrong operation, and C divides square feet by square inches.
