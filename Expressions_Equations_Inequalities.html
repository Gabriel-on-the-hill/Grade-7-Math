1 · Building & Combining Like Terms

An expression is built from terms. Before you can solve anything, you learn to tidy an expression up.

By the end: you can name the parts of an expression and combine like terms.

The idea

A term is a single piece of an expression, separated by + or −. In 4x, the number multiplying the variable is the coefficient; a number on its own is a constant.

Like terms have the exact same variable part, so they can be combined — add their coefficients, keep the variable. Constants combine with constants.

Worked example

4x + 2 + 3x + 5: the 4x and 3x are like terms and the 2 and 5 are like terms → 7x + 7. You cannot combine 4x with 5, because 4x is not a plain number.

Careful: only the coefficients change when you combine — the variable itself never gets added to a constant.

2 · The Distributive Property — Expand & Factor

The distributive property lets you rewrite a product as a sum, and a sum back as a product.

By the end: you can expand a(b + c) and factor a common number back out.

The idea

Expand: multiply the outside number by every term inside — a(b + c) = ab + ac. A negative outside multiplies each inside term (watch both signs).

Factor: the reverse. Pull the greatest common factor out front — ab + ac = a(b + c).

Worked example

Expand 3(x + 4) → 3x + 12. Factor 6x + 15 → the GCF of 6 and 15 is 3, so 3(2x + 5).

3 · Rewriting to Reveal Structure

The same amount can be written two ways — and one way often tells the story better.

By the end: you can rewrite “a price plus/minus a percent of itself” as a single multiplier.

The idea

A price p increased by 5% is p plus 5% of p: p + 0.05p. Combining like terms (think of p as 1p) gives 1.05p — the single number that scales the price.

A price decreased by 20% is p − 0.20p = 0.80p. The multiplier is what is left: 100% − 20% = 80%.

Worked example

A 6% tax on p: p + 0.06p = 1.06p. To pay 6% on top, you multiply the price by 1.06.

4 · From Words to Equations

The hardest part of a word problem is usually the translation, not the arithmetic.

By the end: you can turn a described situation into an expression or equation.

The idea

Name the unknown with a letter. Then translate piece by piece: “per” or “each” → multiply; “more than” / “plus” → add; “is” → =.

Order matters. “5 more than twice a number” is 2n + 5, not 2(n + 5) — double first, then add 5.

Worked example

“A gym charges a $20 fee plus $10 per month for m months.” Cost = 10m + 20. If the total is $80, the equation is 10m + 20 = 80.

5 · Solving Two-Step Equations

To solve is to undo. You peel the operations off the variable in reverse order.

By the end: you can solve px + q = r and p(x + q) = r equations and check your answer.

The idea

Whatever you do to one side, do to the other. For px + q = r: first undo the q (add or subtract), then undo the p (divide). For p(x + q) = r: you may divide by p first, or distribute first — both reach the same answer.

A bar model shows the structure before you touch the symbols: the whole bar is r, one part is the constant q, and the rest is p equal x-parts. Taking q off both sides and splitting what is left into equal parts is “undo q, then undo p.”

Worked example — 3x + 4 = 19

Subtract 4 → 3x = 15; divide by 3 → x = 5. Check: 3(5) + 4 = 19 ✓

6 · Inequalities — Solve & Graph

An inequality has many answers, not one. You solve it almost like an equation — with a single, crucial exception.

By the end: you can solve px + q > r, graph the solution, and flip the sign when you must.

The idea

Solve an inequality with the same moves as an equation. On a number line, an open circle means the boundary is not included (< or >); a closed circle means it is (≤ or ≥). Shade toward every number that works.

The one rule that is different: when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, the inequality sign flips. −2x > 6 becomes x < −3.
Worked example — graph of x > 3

x > 3: every number bigger than 3, not 3 itself → open circle at 3, arrow to the right.
