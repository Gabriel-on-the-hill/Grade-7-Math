1 · Ratios, Rates & Unit Rates

A ratio compares two quantities; a unit rate tells you the amount per one.

By the end: you can find unit rates, including with fractions, and compare rates.

The idea

A rate compares quantities with different units; a unit rate is the rate for exactly 1 (divide).

Worked example

120 miles in 3 hours: 120 ÷ 3 = 40 miles per hour.

2 · Proportional Relationships

Two quantities are proportional when one is always a constant times the other.

By the end: you can spot a proportional relationship and find its constant of proportionality.

The constant of proportionality

If y/x is the same for every pair, the relationship is proportional with constant k, and y = kx.

Worked example

x: 2, 4, 6 and y: 6, 12, 18 → 6/2 = 12/4 = 18/6 = 3, so k = 3 and y = 3x.

3 · Graphs of Proportional Relationships

A proportional relationship graphs as a straight line through the origin.

By the end: you can read the constant of proportionality and unit rate from a graph.

Reading the graph

  • Straight line through (0,0) → proportional.
  • The point (1, r) shows the unit rate r.
  • k = y/x for any point on the line.
Worked example — reading k off a graph

A machine fills bottles at a steady rate. Its graph is a straight line through the origin passing through (3, 12).

1. Pick any point on the line: (3, 12). 2. k = y/x = 12/3 = 4 bottles per second. 3. Check it against the point at x = 1: (1, 4) — the unit rate is always the y-value when x = 1.

(0, 0) is on every proportional graph, and it means what you expect: in 0 seconds the machine fills 0 bottles.

ExamMCAP · which graph has k = ½
MCAP item · Which graph represents a proportional relationship from y to x with a constant of proportionality of ½? Click the graph that shows it.
A constant of proportionality of ½ means y = ½x — two steps right for every one step up, so the line passes through (2, 1), (4, 2) and (6, 3). The two steep lines are k = 2 and k = 3 (the reciprocal trap — that is x = ½y, not y = ½x), and the shallowest is k = ⅓.

6 · Percent Applications

Tax, tip, discount, markup, markdown, commission, fees, simple interest, percent change and percent error.

Before this section: it leans on one move from Expressions, Equations & Inequalities — rewriting p + 0.2p as 1.2p, so a percent change becomes a single multiplier. If that is unfamiliar, do section 3 of that unit first; everything here will be one step instead of two.

By the end: you can solve multi-step money problems with percents.

Key moves

  • Tax/tip/markup add on; discount takes off.
  • Simple interest: I = P × r × t.
  • Percent change = (change ÷ original) × 100.
  • Percent error = (|estimate − actual| ÷ actual) × 100.
  • Commission is a percent of sales; a fee adds on like tax.
Worked example — “20% more” as one multiplier

A package held 500 grams of cereal. The new one has 20% more. “20% more” means 20% of the original has been added:

500 + (0.2) × 500 = 600 grams.

And because the original is itself 100%, the new amount is 120% of it:

500 + (0.2) × 500 = (1 + 0.2)500

That is the same move you met in Expressions: p + 0.2p = 1.2p. One multiplier, one step.

For tax it works identically: $50 with 8% tax is 50 × 1.08 = 54.

ApplyAgree or disagree?
Q1 · “Employee A gets a pay raise of 50%. Employee B gets a pay raise of 45%. So Employee A gets the bigger pay raise.” Do you agree?
A percentage is always a percentage of something. 50% of $20,000 is $10,000, but 45% of $60,000 is $27,000. Without the starting salaries the comparison cannot be made — though it is fair to say A’s raise is the bigger proportion.
Q2 · Shirts are 20% off. You buy two. The cashier says “20% off each shirt means 40% off the total.” Do you agree?
Two shirts at $30 each cost $60; at 20% off, each costs $24, so the total is $48. That is $12 off $60 — still 20%. Discounts apply to each price, so buying more never deepens the rate.

ExamExam-style · find the error
A student says the table x: 2, 4, 6 with y: 5, 10, 16 is proportional, “because y goes up whenever x goes up.” What is wrong with that reasoning?
5/2 = 2.5 and 10/4 = 2.5, but 16/6 ≈ 2.67. A relationship can increase steadily and still not be proportional — the constant ratio is the test, not the direction.

MCAP item · Which statement is true about the work?
Every step holds. 25% off means you pay 75%, and 75% = 3/4 — so 0.25 would be the discount, not the multiplier. Tax adds, so 1 + 0.08 is right and 0.92 would subtract it.
